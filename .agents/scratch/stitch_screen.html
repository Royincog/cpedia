The History of Library Science

From the series: Intellectual Cartography • Reading time: 14 mins

Library science, often termed as Library and Information Science (LIS), is an interdisciplinary field that applies the practices, perspectives, and tools of management, information technology, education, and other areas to libraries. The history of library science is inextricably linked to the history of human civilization itself—as long as people have recorded information, they have sought methods to organize and preserve it.

Fig 1.1: The aesthetic preservation of knowledge within the monastic tradition.

Early History

The earliest libraries consisted of archives of the earliest form of writing—the clay tablets in cuneiform script discovered in Sumer, some dating back to 2600 BC. These proto-libraries marked the first attempts at bibliographic organization. In Ancient Egypt, the Great Library of Alexandria represented the peak of early library science, attempting to house the "world's knowledge" through systematic collection and translation of texts from across the Mediterranean and beyond.

Scholars at Alexandria introduced early forms of cataloging, arranging scrolls by subject matter and author, a practice that would lay the foundations for modern taxonomic systems.

The Middle Ages

During the Middle Ages, the responsibility for knowledge preservation shifted to monastic communities. Libraries in monasteries across Europe and the Islamic world became the primary repositories of ancient wisdom. The House of Wisdom in Baghdad served as a crucial intellectual hub, where scholars engaged in the translation and preservation of Greek, Persian, and Indian texts.

This era saw the development of the "chained library," where books were literally chained to desks to prevent theft, reflecting both the immense value and scarcity of manuscripts before the advent of the printing press.

Modern Theories

The 19th and 20th centuries saw the professionalization of the field. Melvil Dewey’s Dewey Decimal Classification (1876) revolutionized how books were organized, moving from fixed-location shelf systems to relative-location subject systems. This allowed libraries to grow dynamically without needing to re-catalog entire collections.

The Digital Era

In the late 20th century, the shift from physical to digital information transformed library science once again. The role of the librarian evolved from a curator of physical objects to a "knowledge architect," managing complex databases, digital rights, and open-access repositories. The contemporary library is as much a digital node as it is a physical space.
